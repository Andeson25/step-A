Step A testing service
- a new initiative

Welcome to this learning module on the new Strep A testing service available to customers. The service allows a trained pharmacist to assess a customer presenting with sore throat to determine if they are suffering from a streptococcal throat infection. Following a swab test, if the customer shows a positive result for the presence of Strep A, the trained pharmacist can supply antibiotics for the treatment of the infection, if appropriate, via an in-store Patient Group Direction (PGD).

Content developed by CIG Healthcare Partnership.

  • Describe how to undertake the Strep A swab test
  • Explain what to do in the event of a positive, negative or invalid test result
  • Detail important advice to give to customers if an antibiotic is supplied via the PGD

Sore throat and Strep A


Sore throat is a common condition which can be due to a viral or bacterial cause. Both are normally a self-limiting and generally resolve within two weeks.* An example of a bacterial cause of sore throat is streptococcal infection.


Streptococcal bacteria are highly contagious. They can spread through airborne droplets when someone with the infection coughs or sneezes, or through droplets on hard surfaces such as door handles, toys or cups.


Group A streptococcus infections (known as Strep A) are characterised by pain at the back of the throat which feels worse when swallowing, along with redness in the throat and possibly puss on the tonsils along with a fever of over 38.5°C. Children may also have a rash. Although Strep A is not considered dangerous, it can lead to complications including scarlet fever, rheumatic fever and inflammation of the kidneys.


Always keep the above in mind when speaking to customers about their symptoms so you can provide the appropriate advice.

* Georgalas et al, 2014; Kenealy, 2014; BMJ Best Practice, 2016a

Important

You will be swabbing a potentially infectious patient, therefore appropriate measures must be taken to protect yourself and others. You should wear both disposable latex free gloves and wash your hands both before and after the procedure.


The tongue depressor must be single use only and all waste must be disposed of in clinical waste.

The FeverPAIN score

The FeverPAIN criteria is a clinical scoring tool that can help to identify if a customer’s sore throat is likely to be as a result of a Strep A infection. The criteria is as follows:

  • Fever during previous 24 hours
  • Purulence (pus on tonsils)
  • Attend rapidly (within 3 days after onset of symptoms)
  • Severely Inflamed tonsils
  • No cough or coryza (inflammation of mucus membranes in the nose)

Each of the FeverPAIN criteria scores 1 point (maximum score of 5). A score of 4 or 5 is thought to be associated with a 62-65% likelihood of isolating streptococcus and would deem the customer eligible for the service. You can now undertake the swab test.


If the customer doesn't meet the criteria, you could recommend an OTC product (if appropriate) or refer them to their GP if they are worried about their symptoms.


You should also take the customers pulse – anyone with a pulse of over 100 needs to see a doctor immediately or be referred to A&E.

Undertaking the swab test

When collecting the specimen from customers, you will need to ask them to sit upright, facing a strong light source, tilt their head back, open their mouth and stick their tongue out.

Obtaining the results

Carefully follow the steps below after collecting the specimen to obtain the results of the test. Make sure you carefully read the test kit instruction leaflet before undertaking the test:

Step 1

Add four drops of Reagent A and then four drops of Reagent B. In order to ensure reliable drop size when adding the reagents, hold the dropper bottles vertically.

Step 2

Immediately insert specimen swab and vigorously mix the solution at least 5 times

Step 3

Let stand for 1-2 minutes before removing swab from test tube (the solution should turn yellow)

Step 4

Remove a test strip from the sealed foil pouch, hold at the marked end and immerse it vertically into the tube. In order to avoid contamination, do not touch the membrane of the test strip, and make sure not to immerse the test strip beyond the maximum immersion mark (MAX).


Leave the test strip in the tube. Alternatively, remove the test strip from the tube after one minute and place it on a dry, clean surface.


As the test begins to run, you will observe a coloured liquid migrate along the membrane.


The test result should be read after 5 minutes. Do not interpret the result after more than 10 minutes.

Dispensing the medication via the PGD

If there is a positive result and the customer meets the other inclusion criteria, you can now supply phenoxymethylpenicillin tablets (or clarithromycin if penicillin allergic) to the customer via the PGD. Click on the hotspots below for details on the counselling to provide to customers.

Q1

Explain the nature, purpose and risks of the antibiotic – refer to the summary of product characteristics. Ensure that you use language that the customer will understand and put them at ease.

Q2

Ensure that you get written and verbal consent from the customer who is using the service. An eligibility questionnaire and antibiotic checklist will be used to determine eligibility for receiving phenoxymethylpenicillin (e.g. if they are penicillin allergic) and ensure customer consent.

Q3

If the customer asks questions that you cannot answer, refer them to their GP and do not supply the antibiotic

Q4

Ensure the patient information leaflet is available and offered to the customer and that you advise on dosage requirements and common side effects within the leaflet or on the PGD.

Q5

If the customer feels very unwell or experiences severe or persistent side effects, advise the customer to see a doctor immediately and call 999/go to A&E in an emergency.

Q6

Advise the customer on the importance of completing the full antibiotic course and encourage them to inform their GP of the consultation following antibiotic treatment.
